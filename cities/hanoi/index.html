Hanoi is not a city you visit. It is a city that enters you.

With over 1,000 years of history as Vietnam's capital, Hà Nội moves at a rhythm that honors the past while racing toward the future. This is where Vietnamese civilization found its voice—and where it speaks most eloquently today.

Founded 1010 CE

The City of a Thousand Years

Founded in 1010 CE by Emperor Lý Thái Tổ, who named it Thăng Long (Rising Dragon) after seeing a golden dragon ascending from the Red River, Hanoi has served as Vietnam's political, cultural, and spiritual center for a millennium.

This is not a city built for tourists. It is a city built for Vietnamese life—and visitors are simply invited to witness it. The chaos of motorbikes, the 5 AM phở shops, the grandmothers doing tai chi by Hoan Kiem Lake—all of it forms a living organism that has survived Chinese occupation, French colonization, American bombing, and rapid modernization.

Phố Cổ

The Old Quarter: 36 Streets

A maze of medieval commerce where each street was dedicated to a specific trade—and the names preserve that history today.

Hàng Bạc

Silver Street

Once home to silversmiths and money changers. Today, jewelry shops line both sides, continuing centuries of metalwork tradition.

Hàng Gai

Hemp/Silk Street

Originally hemp rope, now silk and embroidery. The finest áo dài tailors and lacquerware shops.

Tube Houses

Nhà Ống

Narrow at the front but deep—designed when taxes were based on street frontage. Vietnamese architectural ingenuity.

Sacred Spaces

The Soul of Hanoi

The Heart

Hồ Hoàn Kiếm

Lake of the Returned Sword

Legend says Emperor Lê Lợi received a magical sword from the lake's golden turtle to defeat Chinese invaders, then returned it here. Whether myth or history, the lake functions as Hanoi's communal living room—where elderly couples stroll at dawn, children play on weekends, and young lovers meet at night.

The small Ngọc Sơn Temple on an island, reached by the iconic red Thê Húc Bridge, houses a preserved giant turtle—a reminder that the legend contains truth wrapped in poetry.

Founded 1070

Văn Miếu

Temple of Literature

Vietnam's first national university—dedicated to Confucius and education. For nearly 700 years, it trained the scholars who ran the empire. Today, students come before exams to pray for good results.

National Pilgrimage

Lăng Bác

Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum

Uncle Ho's final resting place—against his wishes for a simple cremation. Vietnamese come from across the country to pay respects. The stilt house where he actually lived sits nearby.

Culinary Capital

The Hanoi Table

Hanoi is Vietnam's culinary capital—not for variety, but for depth. Northern food is subtle, elegant, and fiercely debated.

The Original

Phở Hà Nội

Phở was born in Hanoi, and Hanoians will tell you—with absolute conviction—that northern phở is the only true phở. Clearer broth, less sweet, minimal garnishes. The beef is the star.

Hanoi Signature

Bún Chả

Grilled pork with rice noodles—made famous worldwide after Obama and Bourdain shared a meal here. The smoky char, the sweet-sour dipping sauce, the pile of fresh herbs.

Invented Here

Cà Phê Trứng

Invented in Hanoi when milk was scarce, this rich, custard-like coffee has become the city's signature drink. Egg yolk whipped with condensed milk, floated on dark roast.

So Famous It Has a Street

Chả Cá

Turmeric-marinated fish, fried tableside with dill and green onions. Chả Cá Street in the Old Quarter is named entirely after this single dish.

Experiencing Hanoi

To understand Hanoi, you must surrender to its rhythm:

  • Wake early. The city is most alive at 6 AM—phở sellers, exercise groups, quiet before the motorbike flood.
  • Walk the Old Quarter. Get lost. That's the point.
  • Sit by Hoan Kiem Lake. Watch Hanoi be Hanoi.
  • Eat like a local. Tiny plastic chairs, street-side, no menus.
  • Visit the temples. Not as a tourist, but as a witness.
  • Stay for the weekend. Saturday nights, the streets become pedestrian-only.

Hanoi Questions

Is Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City better to visit?

They offer completely different experiences. Hanoi is older, more traditional, and moves at a contemplative pace—ideal for history lovers and those seeking Vietnamese cultural depth. Ho Chi Minh City (Saigon) is younger, faster, more chaotic and entrepreneurial—better for those who want energy and modern Vietnam. Most visitors benefit from seeing both.

What is Hanoi known for?

Hanoi is known for: being Vietnam's capital for over 1,000 years; the Old Quarter with its 36 ancient streets; Hoan Kiem Lake and its legend; the Temple of Literature (Vietnam's first university); the best phở in Vietnam; bún chả; egg coffee (cà phê trứng); French colonial architecture; Ho Chi Minh's Mausoleum; and a pace of life that honors Vietnamese tradition.

What does Hanoi mean in Vietnamese?

Hà Nội (河內) means "Inside the Rivers" or "Between the Rivers"—referring to the city's position within the Red River system. The name was given in 1831. Before this, the city was called Thăng Long (Rising Dragon) for over 800 years, named by Emperor Lý Thái Tổ in 1010 CE.

When is the best time to visit Hanoi?

The best time is October to December (autumn) and March to April (spring)—pleasant temperatures, less rain, clear skies. Summer (May-August) is hot and humid with afternoon storms. Winter (December-February) can be surprisingly cold and drizzly. Avoid Tết (Lunar New Year) unless you want to experience the holiday—many businesses close.

Is Hanoi safe for tourists?

Hanoi is very safe by global standards. Violent crime against tourists is rare. The main concerns are traffic (crossing streets requires confidence—walk slowly and steadily, don't stop suddenly), petty theft (watch your phone and bag in crowded areas), and scams (agree on prices before services, use reputable tour operators). The biggest danger is honestly the traffic.
