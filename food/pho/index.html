National Dish

Pho

Vietnam's Soul in a Bowl

The Dish

More Than Soup

Pho (pronounced "fuh") is Vietnam's most famous dish—a fragrant, deeply flavorful noodle soup that has conquered the world while remaining deeply rooted in Vietnamese daily life.

The magic lies in the broth, simmered for hours with beef bones, charred onions and ginger, star anise, cinnamon, and cloves. Rice noodles swim beneath thin slices of beef (pho bo) or chicken (pho ga), topped with fresh herbs you add tableside.

Every Vietnamese has their pho loyalty—Hanoi style (subtle, clear broth) or Saigon style (sweeter, with more herbs and sauces). Both are perfect.

Varieties

Types of Pho

Pho Bo (Beef)

The classic. Choose from rare beef (tai), well-done (chin), brisket (gau), tendon (gan), or mix it up.

Most Popular

Pho Ga (Chicken)

Lighter broth with tender poached chicken. Often considered easier on the stomach.

Lighter Option

Hanoi Style

Subtle, clear broth with fewer toppings. Purists say this is the "real" pho.

Northern

Saigon Style

Sweeter broth with abundant herbs, bean sprouts, hoisin, and sriracha on the side.

Southern
Guide

How to Eat Pho

1. Taste First

Try the broth before adding anything. Appreciate the base flavor the cook created.

2. Add Herbs

Tear basil leaves, add bean sprouts, squeeze lime. Build your bowl gradually.

3. Sauce Wisely

Hoisin and sriracha go in a small dish for dipping meat—never directly in the broth (purists say).

4. Use Both Utensils

Chopsticks for noodles and meat, spoon for broth. Slurping is acceptable!
