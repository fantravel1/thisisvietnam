Hanoi Specialty

BunCha

The Dish That Conquered a President

The Dish

Hanoi's Lunchtime Ritual

Every day around 11am, smoke rises from charcoal grills across Hanoi as the city prepares its most beloved dish. Bun cha is more than food—it's a social ritual, a lunchtime meditation, a taste of the capital's soul.

Grilled pork patties and caramelized sliced pork belly arrive in a bowl of warm, sweet-sour dipping broth. You add cold vermicelli noodles, fresh herbs, pickled papaya, and garlic chili to taste. The interplay of smoky, sweet, tangy, fresh, and spicy creates magic.

In 2016, Anthony Bourdain brought President Obama to Bun Cha Huong Lien for a $6 meal. The restaurant now serves the "Obama Combo" and has preserved their table behind glass. The dish went global, but it remains quintessentially Hanoian.

The Components

What's in Bun Cha

Cha Mieng

Hand-formed pork patties grilled over charcoal until charred and caramelized. Seasoned with shallots and fish sauce.

Cha Que

Thin slices of pork belly threaded on skewers, grilled until crispy edges and tender center.

Nuoc Cham

The warm dipping broth—fish sauce, vinegar, sugar, garlic, and chili in perfect balance. This makes or breaks the dish.

Bun

Cold rice vermicelli noodles that you dip into the warm broth, picking up pork and herbs as you go.

Fresh Herbs

Basket of mint, perilla, lettuce, and other greens that add freshness between rich bites of pork.

Nem Cua Be

Optional but recommended—crispy crab spring rolls that complement the meal perfectly.

Where to Eat

Best Bun Cha in Hanoi

Bun Cha Huong Lien

The "Obama Bun Cha" spot. Tourist crowds now, but quality remains good. Visit for the experience.

Bun Cha Dac Kim

Old Quarter institution since 1965. Consistently excellent with devoted local following. Multiple locations.

Bun Cha 34

Tiny shophouse on Hang Than street. No-frills, incredible quality. Come early—they sell out.

Street Stalls

Look for the charcoal grills and crowds of locals at lunchtime. Any popular stall will deliver.
