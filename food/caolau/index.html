CaoLau

The Noodles That Cannot Travel

The Legend

Water, Ash, and Ancient Wells

Cao lau can only be made in Hoi An. Not through legal protection or stubborn tradition, but because the dish requires water from the town's ancient Cham wells and ash from trees on the nearby Cu Lao Cham islands.

The alkaline well water and special ash give the thick rice noodles their distinctive chewy texture and yellow-brown color—something that cannot be replicated elsewhere. This makes cao lau one of Vietnam's most geographically restricted dishes.

The origins are debated: some see Japanese soba influence from 17th-century traders, others Chinese wonton noodles, still others pure Cham heritage. Perhaps it's all three, blended together in this ancient trading port to create something unique.

The Components

What Makes Cao Lau

The Noodles

Thick, chewy rice noodles soaked in lye water from Ba Le well ash. Brown-tinged with firm, almost pasta-like bite.

Char Siu Pork

Chinese-style barbecue pork, sliced thin. Sweet, caramelized edges with tender interior.

Crispy Croutons

Fried pieces of the same noodle dough—crunchy contrast to the soft noodles below.

Fresh Herbs

Bean sprouts, mint, lettuce, and local greens piled high on top.

The Broth

Just a small amount of pork and soy-based sauce—enough to moisten but not submerge. This isn't soup.

Chili & Lime

Added tableside to taste. The lime brightens everything.

Where to Eat

Best Cao Lau in Hoi An

Central Market

Multiple stalls in the morning market serve excellent cao lau. Watch it being made, eat on plastic stools.

Trung Bac

Local institution since 1959. No-frills shop with generations of loyal customers. Cash only.

Thanh Cao Lau

Popular with both locals and tourists. Consistent quality in comfortable setting.

Ba Le Well

Visit the actual ancient well that provides the water for authentic cao lau noodles.
