ComTam

Broken Rice, Unbroken Spirit

The Dish

From Scraps to Saigon Soul

Com tam literally means "broken rice"—the fractured grains that were once considered inferior and sold cheaply. Saigon's working class turned these humble fragments into the city's most beloved comfort food.

The smaller, broken grains cook differently than whole rice—slightly drier with more surface area to absorb the sweet fish sauce (nuoc mam) that's essential to the dish. Topped with a perfectly grilled pork chop (suon nuong), fried egg, shredded pork skin, and pickled vegetables, it's breakfast, lunch, and dinner for millions of Saigonese.

You'll find com tam stalls on nearly every Saigon street corner, each with their own charcoal grill smoking away, filling the air with the irresistible aroma of caramelizing pork.

The Components

Building a Perfect Plate

Suon Nuong

Grilled pork chop marinated in lemongrass, fish sauce, and sugar. Charred edges, tender center. The star of the plate.

Bi

Shredded pork skin mixed with toasted rice powder. Adds chewy texture and subtle flavor.

Cha Trung

Steamed egg meatloaf with pork, glass noodles, and mushrooms. Soft contrast to crispy pork.

Op La

Fried egg with runny yolk that mixes into the rice and fish sauce.

Do Chua

Pickled daikon and carrot that cuts through the richness with bright acidity.

Nuoc Mam

Sweet fish sauce—the essential condiment. Ladle generously over everything.

How to Order

Com Tam Variations

Com Tam Suon

Basic with just grilled pork chop. The purist's choice.

Com Tam Suon Bi Cha

The classic combo—pork chop, shredded skin, and egg meatloaf. Most popular order.

Com Tam Dac Biet

"Special" with everything—pork chop, bi, cha, fried egg, and often more.

Add-Ons

Ask for extra suon (more pork), op la (fried egg), or tom nuong (grilled shrimp).
