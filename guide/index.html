Entry Requirements

Visas & Documentation

Vietnam has significantly relaxed visa requirements in recent years, making it easier than ever to visit this beautiful country.

Visa Exemption (No Visa Needed)

Citizens of 25 countries can enter visa-free for 15-45 days depending on nationality. This includes most EU countries, UK, USA, Japan, South Korea, and ASEAN nations.

E-Visa (Online Application)

Available to citizens of 80+ countries. Apply online at least 3 days before arrival. Valid for 90 days with single or multiple entry options. Cost: ~$25 USD.

Visa on Arrival

Available at international airports with pre-approved letter. Popular for last-minute trips but requires invitation letter from a Vietnamese sponsor or travel agency.

Pro Tip

Always check the latest visa requirements before traveling as policies change frequently. Your passport must be valid for at least 6 months beyond your planned stay.

When to Go

Best Time to Visit

Vietnam's climate varies dramatically from north to south

Northern Vietnam

Best: October - April

Cool, dry winters (Oct-Mar) are ideal for Hanoi and Sapa. Summers (May-Sep) bring heat and monsoon rains. Spring (Feb-Apr) offers blooming flowers and Tet festivities.

Central Vietnam

Best: February - May

Da Nang, Hoi An, and Hue are best in spring. Avoid Sep-Nov when typhoons and flooding are common. Summer (Jun-Aug) is hot but great for beaches.

Southern Vietnam

Best: December - April

Saigon and the Mekong Delta have two seasons: dry (Dec-Apr) and wet (May-Nov). The dry season offers perfect weather; the wet season brings afternoon thunderstorms but lush landscapes.

Year-Round Destination: Vietnam can be visited any time‚Äîyou just need to choose your region wisely. When it's wet in the north, it's often dry in the south!

Getting Around

Transportation Options

Domestic Flights

Vietnam Airlines, VietJet, and Bamboo Airways connect major cities. Hanoi to Saigon takes ~2 hours. Book early for best prices ($30-100 one way).

Best for: Long distances

Trains

The Reunification Express runs the full length of Vietnam (Hanoi-Saigon: 30-40 hours). Scenic but slow. Book soft sleeper berths for comfort.

Best for: Scenic journeys

Buses

Extensive network including sleeper buses for overnight trips. Companies like The Sinh Tourist and Futa Bus are reliable. Very budget-friendly.

Best for: Budget travel

Grab (Ride-Hailing)

The Southeast Asian Uber. Essential app for city travel. Shows fare upfront, no haggling, and works for motorbikes (GrabBike) or cars.

Best for: City transport

Motorbike Rental

Freedom to explore! Rent for $5-10/day. International driving permit technically required. Traffic is chaotic‚Äîonly for experienced riders.

Best for: Adventure seekers

Cyclos & Xe Om

Cyclos (bicycle rickshaws) for leisurely old town tours. Xe Om (motorbike taxis) for quick hops. Agree on price before starting!

Best for: Short trips & experiences
Stay Safe

Health & Safety

Health Precautions

Vaccinations

Recommended: Hepatitis A & B, Typhoid, Tetanus. Consider: Japanese Encephalitis, Rabies (if rural travel). Consult a travel clinic 4-6 weeks before departure.

Water & Food

Don't drink tap water‚Äîbottled water is cheap and everywhere. Street food is generally safe if it's cooked fresh and hot. Avoid ice in rural areas.

Mosquitoes

Dengue fever exists year-round. Use DEET repellent, especially at dawn/dusk. Malaria risk is low in cities but present in remote highland areas.

Medical Care

Major cities have international-standard hospitals. Travel insurance with medical evacuation coverage is strongly recommended.

Safety Tips

General Safety

Vietnam is very safe for tourists. Violent crime against visitors is rare. Use common sense and you'll be fine.

Petty Theft

Bag snatching from motorbikes occurs in cities. Wear bags cross-body, walk facing traffic, don't flash expensive items. Keep valuables in hotel safes.

Scams to Avoid

Overcharging taxis (use Grab instead), fake tour agencies, "tea ceremony" scams, rigged cyclo meters. Research prices beforehand.

Traffic

Crossing the street is an art form. Walk slowly and steadily‚Äîtraffic will flow around you. Never stop suddenly or run. The chaos has a rhythm.

Etiquette

Cultural Tips

DO

Remove shoes

Take off shoes when entering homes, temples, and some shops. Follow the lead of locals.

DON'T

Touch heads

The head is considered sacred. Never touch someone's head, even children.

DO

Dress modestly at temples

Cover shoulders and knees when visiting pagodas and temples. Carry a scarf for quick cover-ups.

DON'T

Point with your feet

Feet are considered the lowest part of the body. Never point your feet at people or religious objects.

DO

Use both hands

Give and receive items (especially business cards, money, gifts) with both hands as a sign of respect.

DON'T

Public displays of affection

Vietnamese culture is conservative. Keep romantic displays private, especially in rural areas.

DO

Bargain respectfully

Haggling is expected at markets. Start at 50% of asking price, be friendly, and walk away if needed.

DON'T

Lose your temper

Raising your voice or showing anger causes loss of face for everyone. Stay calm and smile through frustrations.
