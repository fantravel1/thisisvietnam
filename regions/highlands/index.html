The Hidden Region

Vietnam's Frontier

The Tay Nguyen—Vietnam's Central Highlands—remains one of the country's least-visited regions by international travelers. This vast plateau of red basalt soil, pine forests, and coffee plantations is where Vietnam feels most wild and undiscovered.

Forty-seven ethnic minority groups call these highlands home, including the Jarai, Ede, Bahnar, and Sedang peoples. Their longhouses, gong music, and animist traditions create a cultural landscape found nowhere else in Vietnam.

This is also Vietnam's coffee heartland. The country is the world's second-largest coffee exporter, and most of it grows here—endless green rows of robusta plants stretching across the rolling hills, ready to become your morning ca phe sua da.

Highland Destinations

Three provinces, endless discoveries

Dak Lak Province

Buon Ma Thuot

The coffee capital of Vietnam. Visit plantations, learn roasting techniques, and taste single-origin brews at the source. Don't miss Dray Nur waterfall.

Coffee Hub
Gia Lai Province

Pleiku

Visit volcanic crater lakes, the stunning Bien Ho (Sea Lake), and authentic Jarai villages. Gateway to the legendary Kon Tum further north.

Volcanic Lakes
Kon Tum Province

Kon Tum

The most traditional highlands town. Visit the wooden cathedral, Bahnar villages, and the remarkable communal rong houses that tower above villages.

Cultural Heart
Heritage

Ethnic Cultures

Gong Culture

UNESCO Masterpiece of Intangible Heritage. Bronze gongs accompany all ceremonies—births, funerals, harvests, and buffalo sacrifices.

Longhouses

Traditional Ede and Jarai homes stretch 100+ meters long, housing extended families. Some villages welcome overnight homestays.

Rong Houses

Bahnar communal houses rise 15-20 meters on stilts with soaring thatched roofs—architectural masterpieces at the heart of each village.

Jar Burials

The Jarai practice unique burial customs with elaborate tomb houses. Visiting these sites offers insight into animist beliefs still practiced today.

Can Wine

Rice wine fermented in ceramic jars, drunk through bamboo straws at festivals. Join a village celebration for the authentic experience.

Village Homestays

Sleep in traditional longhouses, share meals with families, and witness daily life unchanged for generations. The most authentic cultural immersion.

Bean to Cup

Coffee Culture

The birthplace of Vietnamese coffee

Vietnam produces nearly 2 million tons of coffee annually, 95% of it robusta grown in the Central Highlands. The French introduced coffee here in the 1850s, but it was the Highlands' volcanic soil and perfect climate that made Vietnam a coffee superpower.

Plantation Tours

Visit working farms, see processing methods, and taste beans at different roast levels

Specialty Coffee

New-wave roasters producing world-class specialty arabica in Buon Ma Thuot and Da Lat

Coffee Museum

The Trung Nguyen Coffee Museum showcases the history and culture of Vietnamese coffee

Plan Your Visit

Practical Information

Best Time to Visit

November-April is dry season with cool temperatures. March sees coffee harvest. Avoid May-October rainy season when roads can be challenging.

Getting There

Fly to Buon Ma Thuot or Pleiku from Saigon/Hanoi. Bus from Nha Trang (5 hrs) or Da Lat (4 hrs). Motorbike loops from Da Lat are popular.

Getting Around

Rent a motorbike (essential for village visits) or hire a car with driver. Public transport is limited. Consider guided tours for ethnic village visits.

How Long to Stay

3-5 days minimum for one province. A week+ for the full highlands experience combining Buon Ma Thuot, Pleiku, and Kon Tum.
